# Heliobiological Resonance: How Space Weather Affects the Human Body

The idea that the Sun influences more than climate and seasons is older than modern science. But when Russian scientist Alexander Chizhevsky first systematically described correlations between sunspot cycles and epidemics, social unrest, and biological rhythms in the 1920s, the scientific establishment dismissed him — and Soviet authorities sent him into exile. A century later, research has vindicated him on a key point: the Sun does influence biological systems on Earth. The remaining questions are how exactly, how strongly, and in whom.

Welcome to heliobiology — the science of solar activity's biological effects.

---

## What Is Heliobiology?

Heliobiology studies interactions between solar activity and biological systems. It is an interdisciplinary field connecting astrophysics, geophysics, epidemiology, neurology, and chronobiology. Its subject is not the obvious effects of sunlight and warmth, but subtler influences: electromagnetic fields, geomagnetic disturbances, cosmic radiation, and ionospheric modulation.

The core question: does the human body respond to changes in the geomagnetic environment triggered by solar activity?

---

## The Solar Cycle: An 11-Year Rhythm

The Sun follows an approximately 11-year activity cycle, from solar minimum to maximum, when sunspots, flares, and coronal mass ejections (CMEs) increase dramatically. We are currently in Solar Cycle 25, peaking around 2024–2026.

During maximum, geomagnetic storm frequency rises significantly. CMEs — massive plasma clouds hurled from the Sun at up to 3,000 km/s — strike Earth's magnetosphere and compress the magnetic field. The resulting disturbance is measured by the Kp index: a scale from 0 (quiet) to 9 (extreme). A geomagnetic storm begins at Kp 5.

---

## The Kp Index and the Human Body

The Kp index is more than a tool for satellite operators and aurora hunters. A growing body of studies correlates Kp values with physiological parameters.

**Autonomic Nervous System (ANS):** The ANS regulates involuntary functions — heart rate, blood pressure, digestion, respiration. Studies show that increased geomagnetic activity shifts the sympathovagal balance toward sympathetic dominance — a "fight-or-flight" signal that raises heart rate and blood pressure.

**Heart Rate Variability (HRV):** HRV measures the variation in time intervals between successive heartbeats. High HRV indicates a flexible, adaptive autonomic nervous system. During geomagnetic storms, HRV drops measurably — sometimes for up to 24 hours after the event. This finding has been replicated across multiple independent studies and ranks among the most robust in heliobiology.

The clinical relevance is clear: chronically low HRV is an established cardiovascular risk factor. The transient HRV reduction during geomagnetic storms may help explain why epidemiological studies find elevated incidence of myocardial infarction and stroke during such periods.

## Cryptochromes: The Protein That "Sees" Magnetic Fields

One of the most fascinating recent breakthroughs concerns the molecular basis of magnetoreception. Cryptochromes are light-sensitive proteins found in the retina and other tissues. They play a central role in the circadian clock and — according to increasingly supported hypotheses — in magnetic field perception.

The mechanism is called the **radical pair reaction**: when cryptochrome absorbs light, radical pairs form — molecules with unpaired electrons whose spin states are influenced by external magnetic fields, altering chemical reaction rates. In migratory birds, this mechanism is experimentally well-established for geomagnetic navigation.

In humans, cryptochromes (CRY1 and CRY2) have been identified in the retina. A 2019 study at the California Institute of Technology (published in eNeuro) showed that the human brain responds to rotating magnetic fields with measurable changes in alpha oscillations on EEG — suggesting functional magnetoreception below the threshold of conscious awareness.

## Case Study: The 2024 Gannon Storm

In May 2024, a G5 storm struck Earth — the strongest geomagnetic storm since 2003, named after NOAA meteorologist Shawn Gannon. The Kp index reached 9, and aurora were visible as far south as central Europe and the southern United States.

This storm provided heliobiology with a natural experiment. Numerous research groups collected data on HRV, blood pressure, sleep quality, and emergency department admissions during and after the event. Preliminary reports suggest elevated cardiovascular presentations and sleep disturbances, though full analysis is still underway.

The Gannon storm also illustrates the reality of modern heliobiology: with wearables continuously measuring HRV and sleep, and global health databases available, population-wide real-time analyses are now possible for the first time.

## Magnetosensitivity: Not Everyone Is Equally Affected

A central finding is that not all people respond equally to geomagnetic disturbances. An estimated 10–15% of the population may be classified as "magnetosensitive" — showing measurable physiological responses while the majority show none.

**Gender differences:** Several studies report that women — particularly between ages 31 and 60 — respond more strongly to geomagnetic disturbances than men. The mechanisms are unclear but may involve hormonal modulation of the autonomic nervous system. In one study of myocardial infarction frequency, women in this age group showed up to threefold higher hospital admission rates on geomagnetically disturbed days.

**Pre-existing conditions:** Individuals with cardiovascular risk factors — obesity, hypertension, kidney disease — show significantly higher vulnerability to geomagnetic effects in studies.

**Age:** Older adults with reduced autonomic flexibility and lower baseline HRV appear more susceptible than younger, healthy individuals.

## What Heliobiology Is Not

An honest account must also state what heliobiology is not: it is not proof that every solar storm makes every person ill. It is not astrology in scientific clothing. It is not a justification for medical fatalism.

Heliobiology is a young, interdisciplinary field with robust individual findings but without a fully closed overall picture. Effects are statistical, not deterministic. They affect subgroups, not the entire population. And they are risk factors, not causes.

What it does show: humans do not live in cosmic isolation. We are electromagnetic beings in an electromagnetic universe. And the Sun — 150 million kilometers away — modulates the field in which we exist. That is not esotericism. That is physics.
